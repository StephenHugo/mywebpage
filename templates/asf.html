- Segmentation of Fluorescent Images -
Given a fluorescent image, it is difficult to segment the cell signal from the background for a variety of reasons: noise, focus, debris, type of probe, morphology, type of microscope system, etc. Typically, the intensity of pixels corresponding to the cell signal will be greater than the background, which facilitates basic segmentation using a single threshold. ImageJ has thisbuilt-in, providing several threshold options and allowing the setting of a manual threshold. However, when images are captured, the light reaching the microscope camera will vary depending on the sample and the choice of UV light and exposure time. The figure below shows a 'target boundary' that our eyes can see, then demonstrates the outcome from using a single threshold on images of the same exact cell, taken with different camera settings.
One aspect of my graduate work dealt with automatically determining the cell boundary from a fluorescent image. Automated analysis is important in drug discovery research since it allows the rapid analysis of a large amount of samples with higher speed than human interaction would allow. The favored term is high-throughput, and it generally relies on faster computer algorithms that might be less accurate, rather than more sophisticated algorithms that take longer to process. Ipublished a newsegmentation algorithm that blends both accuracy and computation speed in order to aid research reliant on fluorescent imaging. The figure below demonstrates my algorithm applied to the same set of images as above, showing a higher degree of consistency for most images.
The above results leave some room for improvement, and since graduating I decided to compare my algorithm to others. Instead of benchmarking their performance by the signal-to-noise ratio or some other arbitrary measure, I decided to measure the consistency of morphological cell parameters instead. This type of benchmarking shows the importance of segmentation when assessing the heterogeneity of cell populations, since poor segmentation will introduce a high degree of variation in apparent cell morphology.
